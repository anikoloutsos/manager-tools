# Chapter Lead — Management Tasks

A chapter lead managing 12 engineers across 5 squads (Spotify model). Senior engineers handle technical oversight within squads. ~50% of time is spent as an individual contributor (software engineering). The primary management focus is people development.

**Context:**
- Engineers are leveled Engineer I through VI.
- Growth observations come primarily from the chapter lead's own 1:1s — squad leads don't feed input on individual growth.
- 1:1 notes are kept in OneNote but unstructured; no consistent format.
- Engineers rarely bring up their own growth proactively — the chapter lead must drive it.
- The biggest operational pain point: growth context is lost between 1:1s, making it hard to maintain continuity and act on what each person needs.

---

## 1. People Development *(primary focus)*

This is the core of the role. With 12 engineers across squads, the challenge is maintaining a clear, up-to-date picture of where each person is in their growth and what they need next.

### 1-on-1s
- Weekly or bi-weekly individual check-ins per engineer.
- Three goals for every 1:1, in order:
  1. **Listen** — understand how the engineer is feeling and doing. Wellbeing first.
  2. **Grow** — revisit agreed targets, propose ideas, help them move forward on their development.
  3. **Connect** — build the relationship; not every conversation needs to be about work.
- Keeping notes and following up on action items between sessions.
- The manager drives the growth agenda — engineers rarely bring it up themselves.

### Growth Tracking *(main pain point)*
- Maintaining a per-engineer view of: current level (I–VI), target level, skill gaps, and growth trajectory.
- Identifying what each engineer needs next to keep growing: a specific project type, a stretch assignment, a skill to develop, visibility with stakeholders.
- Tracking progress over time — not just during review cycles but continuously.
- Noticing when someone has stalled or plateaued and proactively acting on it.

**Current broken workflow:**
- Goals are set at the organisational level but are not actively revisited between cycles.
- 1:1 notes exist in OneNote but are unstructured — hard to scan, no clear signal on what was agreed or what needs follow-up.
- Engineers don't bring their growth up proactively; if the manager doesn't raise it, it doesn't happen.
- Growth context is largely held in memory and decays between sessions.
- No lightweight system to see, at a glance, where each of 12 engineers stands and what they need next.

**Note-taking constraints:**
- Less than 2 minutes available after a 1:1 to write notes — capture must be extremely fast.
- A tool that assists during the meeting (not just after) would also be valuable — structured prompts or a lightweight form during the conversation.

**Current growth target theme (2026):**
- Organisational focus is on AI skills — growth targets for most engineers are framed around AI adoption, usage, and development.
- Targets are set top-down as an org-wide theme; the chapter lead's job is to make them concrete and relevant per engineer.

### Skill Gap Identification
- Assessing technical and soft-skill gaps across the team.
- Planning how to close gaps: mentoring, pairing with a senior, training, project rotation, or direct coaching.
- Distributing growth opportunities fairly across the team (not always defaulting to the same high performers).

### Feedback & Coaching
- Providing timely, specific, actionable feedback — not waiting for formal review cycles.
- Coaching on communication, ownership, problem-solving, and dealing with ambiguity.
- Helping engineers navigate difficult situations (conflict with a colleague, struggling with a project, imposter syndrome).

### Performance Reviews
- Formal reviews twice a year (every 6 months).
- Calibrating ratings and leveling decisions (Engineer I–VI) with peers and leadership.
- Translating 6 months of observations into coherent, evidence-based review narratives — for 12 engineers.
- The gap between reviews is long enough that specific examples and growth moments are hard to recall without good notes.

### Onboarding New Hires
- Structuring the first 30/60/90 days: ramp-up projects, squad integration, onboarding buddy assignment.
- Setting initial expectations and starting the growth conversation from day one.

## 2. Team Health & Culture

- **Psychological safety** — Engineers feel safe raising problems, disagreeing, and taking risks.
- **Motivation & morale** — Monitoring energy levels across squads; noticing and addressing burnout signals early.
- **Conflict resolution** — Mediating interpersonal or cross-squad tensions before they escalate.
- **Diversity & inclusion** — Ensuring equitable opportunity distribution and an inclusive environment across squads.
- **Chapter identity** — Maintaining a sense of shared identity and standards across engineers who are embedded in different squads.

---

## 3. Hiring & Staffing

- **Recruiting pipeline** — Writing job descriptions, sourcing and screening candidates, coordinating interview panels.
- **Interview calibration** — Defining and maintaining consistent technical and behavioral evaluation criteria.
- **Hiring decisions** — Leading debriefs and making or strongly influencing final decisions.
- **Headcount planning** — Forecasting needs per squad and advocating for headcount in planning cycles.

---

## 4. Vendor Relationships & Contracts

- **Vendor evaluation** — Assessing tools, platforms, or service providers relevant to the engineering team.
- **Contract discussions** — Participating in or leading negotiations; ensuring commercial terms align with engineering needs.
- **Ongoing vendor management** — Monitoring value delivered by vendors; raising issues or renegotiating when needed.

---

## 5. Stakeholder & Cross-Team Communication

- **Upward communication** — Reporting team health, people risks, and needs to senior leadership.
- **Cross-chapter alignment** — Coordinating with other chapter leads on shared standards, tooling, and hiring.
- **Representing the team** — Advocating for engineers' needs, growth, and contributions in broader forums.

---

## 6. Engineering Standards (chapter-wide)

Senior engineers own technical direction within squads, but the chapter lead is responsible for:

- **Cross-squad consistency** — Ensuring common practices around testing, code quality, and documentation don't diverge too far across squads.
- **Growing technical leaders** — Coaching senior engineers in their role as squad tech leads; helping them develop leadership skills alongside technical ones.
- **Raising the bar** — Identifying and spreading good engineering practices across the chapter.

---

## 7. Administrative

- PTO approvals and leave management.
- Expense approvals.
- Meeting load management — protecting focus time for engineers and for IC work.
- Compliance with HR processes (probation check-ins, mandatory training, etc.).

---

## Key Tensions to Navigate

- **IC vs. manager time** — Staying technically credible while not letting engineering work crowd out people responsibilities.
- **Squad loyalty vs. chapter ownership** — Engineers identify with their squad; the chapter lead must maintain a second layer of belonging and accountability.
- **Scalability of attention** — With 12 people, it's easy for quieter or more self-sufficient engineers to get less attention. Everyone deserves proactive investment.
- **Growth vs. delivery pressure** — Squad leads optimize for delivery; the chapter lead must ensure growth isn't sacrificed for short-term output.
